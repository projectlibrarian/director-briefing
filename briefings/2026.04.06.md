## Confidentiality in Practice: Two Issues Worth Your Attention

*Patron confidentiality comes up in small decisions made every day: what gets posted to social media, what email address the library uses, how new staff are trained. Two situations that have happened at multiple libraries over the past six months show how easy it is to create confidentiality exposure without intending to.*

### Posting Photos That Include Library Card Numbers

> **⚠️ Warning:** Posting photos of patrons with visible library card numbers is a disclosure of protected patron records under CPLR 4509. Remove all card numbers from images before posting online.

A situation that is happening with slightly more frequency is libraries posting photos of children signing their library cards with the full card number visible in the image. When that photo goes online, it is a disclosure of a library record.

**What the law requires.** New York Civil Practice Law and Rules Section 4509 requires that library records containing names or other personally identifying details about library users be kept confidential. They may only be disclosed to the extent necessary for proper library operation, upon the user's consent, or pursuant to a subpoena or court order. A library card number is personally identifying information. Posting it publicly is a violation of that statute, regardless of intent.

The OWWL Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records Policy covers the same ground. Patron personally identifying information, including barcodes and card numbers, must not be transmitted through unauthorized channels or disclosed without proper authorization.

**What to do.** If your library photographs children signing their cards, the card number must not be visible in any image posted online. Cover it, turn it over, or crop it before posting. This applies to adults as well.

### Posting Photos of Patrons Using the Library

Posting any photo of patrons inside the library raises a separate concern. Legal guidance developed for New York libraries holds that any information indicating an individual's use of library services and facilities should be treated as a library record under CPLR 4509. This is the same framework that applies when law enforcement asks whether a patron was present in the building, and why security camera footage is treated as protected information.

A photo of a patron using the library reveals that the person was there. Posting it publicly discloses that information.

**Two separate legal issues apply to patron photos:**

| Issue | Legal basis |
|---|---|
| Using a person's image for library communications | New York Civil Rights Law Section 50 — requires written consent |
| Disclosing that a person used library services | CPLR 4509 — requires separate patron consent |

A parent signing a standard photo release addresses image rights under Section 50 only. It does not address the CPLR 4509 question. Both must be covered.

This is one reason every library should have a written confidentiality of library records policy. A written policy gives staff a clear framework for decisions about patron information, including photos, and makes these situations easier to navigate consistently.

> **Important:** A photo release form alone is not sufficient. Consent to post patron photos must also cover the disclosure of patron library use under CPLR 4509. If your library posts patron photos without a consent form that covers both points, stop until you have one. Contact your library's counsel or reach out to OWWL if you are unsure whether your current form covers both.

### Using Gmail to Handle Patron Information

> **Issue:** Libraries have used Gmail accounts to handle patron communication, sometimes including patron barcodes or account information in those messages.

This creates a confidentiality problem that is easy to overlook because Gmail feels like an ordinary tool.

**Why Gmail is a problem.** The OWWL Systems Access and Confidentiality of Library Records Policy is clear: patron personally identifying information may not be transmitted through third-party services. Google services, including Gmail, are explicitly identified as third-party platforms. Patron barcodes are personally identifying information protected under CPLR 4509.

There is an additional concern beyond policy. Google scans email content for a range of purposes, including advertising and service improvement. When a patron barcode or other account information appears in a Gmail message, that content may be processed by Google's systems. That processing could constitute a data breach under the confidentiality protections that apply to library patron records.

**The risk ladder.** It helps to think about this as a sequence of escalating risk:

| Step | What happens | Risk level |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | A patron emails their own barcode to the library's Gmail address | Policy concern — barcode now in a third-party inbox |
| 2 | A staff member forwards that barcode in a support ticket or reply | Policy violation and potential confidentiality breach |
| 3 | A staff member replies to a patron hold inquiry with Evergreen data through Gmail | Significant CPLR 4509 violation |
| 4 | A staff member pulls patron data from Evergreen and sends it through Gmail | Confirmed data breach — potential loss of Evergreen subsidy |

The solution is that libraries should not use Gmail accounts for patron communication without an appropriate confidentiality policy in place. Evergreen data, including patron barcodes, hold information, and account details, may not be included in Gmail or any email account other than OWWL Mail. If your library lists a Gmail address on its website, patrons are being invited to send account questions through a third party. Replace it with your OWWL-managed email address.

New staff need explicit training on this. A staff member who grew up using Gmail may not understand why routing patron information through that account is a problem. That is a training gap, not a character flaw, and it is fixable.

## System Direct Access Plan 2027–2031: Open for Comment

**Deadline: April 30, 2026**

OWWL is seeking feedback from member library directors and boards on the draft Direct Access Plan for 2027–2031 before it moves forward for formal approval.

**What is the Direct Access Plan?** It is a required component of the OWWL System's Plan of Service. It governs how library services are made available to all residents within the System's boundaries, including those living outside any member library's chartered service area. It also outlines the conditions under which member libraries may request modifications to unrestricted direct access.

**What's new in this draft.** This draft includes several updates from the current 2022–2026 plan:

- A new negotiation procedure that requires the System to work with municipalities on funding solutions before any restrictions can be imposed
- Updated language affirming the System's role in supporting equitable access
- A new commitment to produce a report identifying unserved areas across Ontario, Wayne, Wyoming, and Livingston Counties

Directors and boards are asked to review the draft and share feedback. The current 2022–2026 plan is available for comparison.

> **Note:** No board action is needed at this time. Once feedback has been reviewed and incorporated, the OWWL Board of Trustees will approve the plan. Following that, a majority of member library boards will need to approve the plan before submission to the Division of Library Development. Separate instructions will follow when that step is ready.

Questions? Contact Ron at [rkirsop@owwl.org](mailto:rkirsop@owwl.org).

[Review the Draft Plan](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C67vyBlvrCxyTMZmHOaJ5gBBN8f1d0j0IUiHYlp5m_w/edit?usp=sharing) — [Submit Feedback](https://forms.gle/A9xz7BbAxxLXqZaD8)
